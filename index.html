AN OVERVIEW OF THE AUSTRALIAN WHEAT INDUSTRY

Wheat is a common grain crop used to make all kinds of foods, from pasta to bread to biscuits, with millions of tons grown every year around the world. Australia is a largescale agricultural producer, and one of its biggest products in the industry is wheat.

Introduced to Australia in 1788 with the arrival of the first fleet, it was initially difficult to grow due to climate and soil differences. However, following artificial selection , it soon became a successful crop. Today, over 30,000 farmers grow it every year, and is worth 15% of Australia's agriculture production- Australia's most valuable crop.

Australian Statistics

Yields by State

Australia is the second-driest continent after Antarctica, and soils are generally nutrient poor, making the majority of it's land unsuitable for intensive agriculture and yields poorer than other countries around the world.

However, Australia's land has become more productive over time due to increased fertiliser use and advances in equipment, reflected in the t/ha produced in each state increasing over time. Tasmania's yield of 6.1 t/ha in 2019 is the highest ever recorded in an Australian state.

Australian Industrial Crop Industry Value

Wheat accounted for almost 75% of all crop production in the mid 1970s. The second biggest crop, barley, paled in comparison, worth less than 15% of the total market.

However producers have since begun diversifying their agriculture, with products such as chickpeas and canola entering the market, gradually reducing the wheat's proportion of Australia's total crop value. This can also be attributed to the recent production of canola due to it's high price and global demand as a result of shortages in other countries.

Wheat has always been Australia's highest value crop by a significant margin, and was worth over 12.3 Billion AUD in 2020. The second most valuable crop produced was canola, which was worth 6 Billion AUD.

The large downward spikes in the Industrial crop sector value are periods of drought, which affected all crop production, including wheat. The millinium drought (2001-2009) was particularly severe and features some of the largest decreases in value.

Area of Wheat Planted

Australia uses a large area to grow it's vast quantities of wheat, larger than South Korea and equivalent to more than half of the area of Victoria.

While land has used to grow wheat has varied over time, production has increased dramatically over time even holding land constant. More than 33,000kt was grown from approximately 13,000 ha in 2020, compared to 20,000kt grown from the same area of land in 1983.

Australian Wheat and the World

Australian Wheat Exports

Australia exports wheat to more than 25 countries around the world on three different continents. Most wheat is exported to South East Asia, with a few African, Middle-Eastern and Pacific countries also receiving shipments each year. The number of countries exported to and total volume has increased dramatically over the last 30 years, with the amount of wheat exported in 1990 less than half of the amount exported in 2020.

Indonesia is Australia's biggest customer, likely due to it's relative proximity and rapidly growing population.

No Australian wheat is exported to North or South America, likely because of the large distance and the fact that there are several large producers there, and so the high cost of shipping from Australia is not justified for the buyers.

Top Global Wheat Producers

In 2020, Australia was the 6th largest producer of wheat, equivalent to 33Mt, one of just 14 countries that grew over 15Mt of wheat that year. Conversely, the world's largest wheat producer, China, grew more than 134kt, more than four times this amount. Despite China's high production, large volumes of Australian wheat are still imported into China each year.

Global Wheat Forecasts

Australian wheat production is predicted to decrease more than any other global region in 2021 and 2023, while reaching similar levels to 2020 in 2020. An explanation for this may be the recent price rises of other agricultural products such as canola and falling wheat prices for wheat, reducing the appeal of growing it in countries like Australia which have suitable conditions for growing many grain crops.
